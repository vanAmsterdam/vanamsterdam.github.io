Some things I found confusing in causal inference: the terms identification, pointing to the concept of finding a unique solution, versus “seeing-is-not-doing”. In settings with unobserved confounding, we know that that marginal outcome rates do not equal causal effects. So seeing is not doing, but the estimate is unique right, even though its wrong. So in what sense is it not identified?

We take Judea Pearl’s definition from the book Causality (Definition 3.2.3 Pearl 2009)

With an example we demonstrate ‘non-identified-ness’

Introduction to example

We consider this simple causal Directed Acyclic Graph over binary variables with with parameterized joint distribution

Parameterizing the example

We follow the causal factorization in parameterizing the joint distribution. First we parameterize \(P(U)\) and \(P(X|U)\)

\[\begin{align} P(U=1) &= p_u \\ P(X=1|U=u) &= (1-u)a_0 + u a_1 \end{align}\]

This leads to the following marginal for \(P(X)\):

\[\begin{align} P(X=1) &= P(X=1|U=0)P(U=0) + P(X=1|U=1)P(U=1) \\ &= a_0 (1-p_u) + a_1 p_u \\ &= p_u (a_1 - a_0) + a_0 \\ P(X=0) &= 1 - P(X=1) \\ &= p_u (a_0 - a_1) - a_0 \end{align}\] {#eq-xmarginals}

And the following \(P(U=1|X=x)\):

\[\begin{align} P(U=1|X=x) = &\frac{P(U=1,X=x)}{P(X=x)} \\ = &\frac{P(X=x|U=1)P(U=1)}{P(X=x)} \\ = &(1-x) \frac{(1-a_1)p_u}{p_u (a_0 - a_1) - a_0} \\ &+x \frac{a_1 p_u}{p_u (a_1 - a_0) + a_0} \\ \end{align}\]

To shorten notation we introduce:

\[\begin{align} \gamma_0 = \gamma_0(p_u,a_0,a_1) &:= \frac{(1-a_1)p_u}{p_u (a_0 - a_1) - a_0} &= P(U=1|X=0) \\ \gamma_1 = \gamma_1(p_u,a_0,a_1) &:= \frac{a_1 p_u}{p_u (a_1 - a_0) + a_0} &= P(U=1|X=1) \end{align}\]

We now parameterize \(P(Y=1|X=x,U=u)\):

\[P(Y=1|X=x,U=u) = q + r x + s u + t xu \tag{1}\]

As in the last conditional all back-door paths are closed, \(P(Y=1|X=x,U) = P(Y=1|\text{do}(X=x),U)\)

The entire distribution is parameterized with 7 parameters:

\[p_u,a_0,a_1,q,r,s,t \tag{2}\]

Assume we have observational data where we do not observe \(U\), this gives us three observations:

\[\begin{align} P(Y=1|X=0) = &P(Y=1|X=0,U=0)P(U=0|X=0) \\ & + P(Y=1|X=0,U=1)P(U=1|X=0) \\ = &(1-\gamma_0) q + \gamma_0 (q + s) \\ = & q + \gamma_0 s \\ P(Y=1|X=1) = &P(Y=1|X=1,U=0)P(U=0|X=1) \\ & + P(Y=1|X=1,U=1)P(U=1|X=1) \\ = &(1-\gamma_1) (q + r) + \gamma_1 (q + r + s + t) \\ = & q + r + \gamma_1 (s + t) \\ P(Y=1) = & P(Y=1|X=0)P(X=0) + P(Y=1|X=1)P(X=1) \\ = & (p_u(a_0 - a_1) - a_0)(q + \gamma_0 s) \\ & + (p_u(a_1 - a_0) + a_0)(q + r + \gamma_1 (s + t)) \end{align}\]

Define:

\[\pi_0 := P(Y=1|X=0) = q + \gamma_0 s \tag{3}\] \[\pi_1 := P(Y=1|X=1) = q + r+ \gamma_1 (s + t) \tag{4}\] \[\pi := P(Y=1) = P(X=0) \pi_0 + P(X=1) \pi_1 \tag{5}\]

In the latter we may choose to express \(P(X=1)\) in terms of \(p_u,a_0,a_1\) when convenient.

Finding parameters with the same marginals but different treatment effects

Looking at the definition of identifiability:

Definition 3.2.3 (Identifiability)

Let \(Q(M)\) be any computable quantity of a model \(M\). We say that \(Q\) is identifiable in a class \(\mathbb{M}\) of models if, for any pairs of models \(M_1\) and \(M_2\) from \(\mathbb{M}\), \(Q(M_1) = Q(M_2)\) whenever \(P_{M_1} (y) = P_{M_2} (y)\). If our observations are limited and permit only a partial set \(F_M\) of features (of \(P_M(y)\)) to be estimated, we define \(Q\) to be identifiable from \(F_M\) if \(Q(M_1) = Q(M_2)\) whenever \(F_{M_1} = F_{M_2}\).

We pick as query the Average Treatment Effect (ATE):

\[\text{ATE} = E[Y|\text{do}(X=1)] - E[Y|\text{do}(X=0)] \tag{6}\]

Are observed features \(F_M\) of the distribution are

\[\pi_1,\pi_0,\pi \tag{7}\]

Can we find values of the parameters in Equation 2 for which the observables Equation 7 are the same, but our target query Equation 6 is not? If that is the case, we have proved (by counter-example) that the target query is not identified from our model assumptions on the DAG in Figure 1 and the observables from the data Equation 7. It turns out, we quite easily can. If we set 4 out of 7 parameters and assume values for the three observables, we can solve for the other three parameters using the equations for the observables. Let’s choose some values for 2 parameters:

\[p_u=0.5, t=0\]

This means the ‘interaction’ term \(t\) for \(X,U\) is zero. And observables:

\[\pi_0=0.3, \pi_1=0.7, \pi = 0.5\]

The fact that \(\pi = 0.5 = P(X=0)\pi_0 + P(X=1)\pi_1\) implies that

\[P(X=1)=0.5 \tag{8}\]

by solving \(0.5 = (1-P(X=1)) 0.3 + P(X=1) 0.5\). Using ?@eq-xmarginals we have that:

\[a_1 + a_0=1 \tag{9}\]

Model 1: no treatment effect

Now we can start adding substantial differences based on confounding and the outcome mechanism. First let’s assume extreme confounding and no causal effect of \(X\) on \(Y\), so:

\[r=0,a_0=0,a_1=1 \tag{10}\]

We have now specified 5 parameters and used one constraint. Working with the equations for \(\pi_0,\pi_1\) we can now solve for \(q,s\). First note that \(\gamma_0 = 0, \gamma_1 = 1\).

We use Equation 3 for \(q\):

\[\pi_0 = q + \gamma_0 s = q = 0.3\]

And Equation 4 for \(s\):

\[\begin{align} \pi_1 &= q + r + \gamma_1 (s+t) = q + s \\ 0.7 &= 0.3 + s \\ &\Rightarrow s = 0.4 \end{align}\]

As \(r=t=0\) we have that our query \(\text{ATE}=0\)

Model 2: no confounding

As an alternative we can say there is no confounding and the observed difference in \(\pi_1,\pi_0\) is thus in fact due to the causal effect of \(X\) on \(Y\), by setting:

\[\begin{align} a_0 = a_1 &= 0.5 \\ q &= 0.3 \\ r &= \pi_1 - \pi_0 = 0.4 \\ s &= 0 \end{align}\]

We can easily verify that these parameters are consistent with the observables and the above definitions of \(p_u,t\) and the observeables.
