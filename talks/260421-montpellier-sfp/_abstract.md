# When accurate prediction models yield harmful self-fulfilling prophecies

Outcome-prediction models are increasingly used to support medical decisions, with the hope of improving care by targeting treatment to patients at highest predicted risk. In this talk, I show that this intuition can fail in a fundamental way. Some prediction models can lead to harmful decisions while still appearing successful after deployment: they harm a subgroup of patients, yet preserve good discrimination because those patients were already predicted to do worse. I present a formal characterization of such harmful self-fulfilling prophecies and use simple examples to illustrate why standard post-deployment predictive performance can be misleading when predictions affect treatment. I then discuss a complementary result: when a model is well calibrated both before and after deployment, this implies that its use did not alter the data-generating process in a way that makes it useful for decision-making. Together, these results argue for a shift in how we evaluate clinical prediction models used in practice: away from predictive performance alone, and toward the treatment policies and patient outcomes they induce.
